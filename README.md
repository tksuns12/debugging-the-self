# Everything Is Just Happening

## An engineer's note after an enlightenment-like realization while driving

While driving, I suddenly realized something simple and strange:

Everything is just happening.

The road was happening.  
The car was happening.  
The trees, sounds, traffic lights, body sensations, and visual field were happening.

But then I noticed that the boundary did not stop there.

My thoughts were also happening.  
My emotions were happening.  
The feeling of “I am thinking” was also happening.  
Even the sense that there is someone inside, watching and controlling the experience, was also just another happening.

There was no special category called “me” outside the system.

The most painful wound in my heart and someone pooping on the other side of the Earth are, in one sense, exactly the same kind of thing:

A happening.

Of course, they are not emotionally the same.  
They are not socially the same.  
They are not morally the same.

One may feel intimate, heavy, and personal.  
The other may feel irrelevant, distant, or even funny.

But before interpretation, before ownership, before “mine” and “not mine,” they are both events appearing in reality.

That realization was strangely freeing.

Not because pain disappeared.  
Not because life became meaningless.  
Not because human suffering stopped mattering.

But because the extra layer of ownership weakened.

Pain could still happen.  
Thoughts could still happen.  
Fear could still happen.

But the belief that “this is absolutely mine, and I am trapped inside it” became less solid.

As a software engineer, the closest analogy I can find is this:

I used to believe I was the main process.

Then I saw that I was also just logs, events, state transitions, and processes arising in the system.

No separate operator.  
No hidden controller.  
Just happening.

## A software engineering analogy

In software, we often separate a system into parts:

- input
- state
- events
- processing
- logs
- side effects
- observers

Before this realization, I unconsciously treated “me” as the observer outside the system.

There was the world, and then there was me watching the world.
There were thoughts, and then there was me thinking them.
There was pain, and then there was me owning it.

But in the realization, the observer was not outside the system anymore.

The observer was also an event.
The feeling of control was also an event.
The thought “I understand this” was also an event.
The thought “this is enlightenment” was also an event.

It was like discovering that what I thought was the root process was actually just another process.

## What changed

The experience did not make me superhuman.
It did not give me perfect peace.
It did not remove ordinary life.

Driving still required attention.
Responsibilities still existed.
Pain could still arise.
Thoughts could still become noisy.

But something in the structure felt different.

Experience became less personal, not in a cold way, but in a lighter way.

Things were still vivid.
Things still mattered.
But they seemed to need less ownership.

A painful memory could appear without becoming an entire identity.
A thought could appear without becoming a command.
A feeling could appear without proving that there was a fixed self behind it.

Everything was just happening.

## Seeing as a whole

There was a teaching I once heard from a Vipassana person.

Someone asked what “from the source” means.

The answer was something like:

> Seeing as a whole is seeing from the source.

At first, this sounded circular.

What does “from the source” mean?  
Seeing as a whole.

What does “seeing as a whole” mean?  
Seeing from the source.

But after this realization, I understood it differently.

Maybe the reason it sounds circular is that the source is not describable as an object in language.

If I describe it as a thing, then that description has already become another happening inside experience.

“Seeing as a whole” means seeing that even “you” are included.

What happens outside is happening.  
What happens inside is happening.  
The thought about the outside is happening.  
The feeling about the inside is happening.  
The sense of being the one who experiences inside is also happening.

So the boundary between inside and outside becomes less solid.

There is no separate inner world owned by “me” standing apart from an outer world called “reality.”

There is just one whole field of happenings.

The seer, the seen, the seeing, the interpretation, the emotion, and the question itself are all included.

That is seeing as a whole.

And maybe that is why it can be called seeing from the source.

## The reader inside the book

Another way the same structure appeared was through a Zen-like question:

> Who is reading the book, when the reader is also part of the book?

Usually, thought assumes there is a reader outside the book.

But if the book is the whole field of experience, then the reader also appears inside it.

The visual field appears.  
The meaning appears.  
The body appears.  
The thought “I am reading” appears.  
The feeling of being the reader appears.

Then the mind assumes:

There should be someone for the book.

There should be someone outside the whole thing, holding it, reading it, or owning it.

But when that someone is searched for, only more experience appears.

The thought about the reader appears.  
The search for the reader appears.  
The sense of ownership appears.  
The pause appears.

The mind tries to step outside the book, but every step is still another sentence in the book.

At that point, thought has nowhere stable to stand.

It does not arrive at a clever answer.
It runs out of the structure that wanted an answer.

Maybe this is why some Zen questions stop thought.
They point back to the one who asks, until the assumed outside observer cannot be found.

There is reading.  
There is the book.  
There is the sense of “me reading.”  
There is the search for the one outside all of it.

But the search itself is also inside the book.

So for a moment, the separation between reader and book collapses.

There is only the whole book reading itself.

## The one and the movie

The “one” cannot be seen or felt as an object.

It is not another thing inside experience.
It is not a special sensation.
It is not something the ego can hold, own, or display.

You can only know its existence indirectly.

Not because it appears as one more object,
but because everything appears within it.

Thoughts appear.
Sensations appear.
The body appears.
The world appears.
The ego appears.
The feeling of being someone appears.
Even the thought “I know the one” appears.

But the one itself is not found as a separate item among these appearances.

If it could be seen, it would be part of the movie.
If it could be felt, it would be another scene.
If it could be grasped, it would be another object for the ego.

So it is known more like the screen is known through the movie.

And when this is recognized, nothing necessarily changes.

The ego is still there.
The body is still there.
Thoughts are still there.
Desires, fears, habits, responsibilities, and ordinary life are still there.

The movie does not disappear.

The shift is simply that the movie is known as a movie.

Before, the character seemed absolutely real.
The story seemed like the final layer of reality.
The ego seemed like the owner of experience.
The world seemed to be happening to someone separate inside it.

Afterward, the same appearances continue,
but they are not believed in exactly the same way.

The ego is not deleted.
It is seen as part of the movie.

Even the recognition itself is part of the movie.

This is why the realization can feel both enormous and strangely ordinary.

Enormous, because the assumed center is no longer solid.
Ordinary, because the dishes still need washing and the body still gets tired.

Nothing changes as content.
Everything changes as context.

The character keeps walking.
The scenes keep moving.
The story keeps unfolding.

But somewhere, quietly, it is known:

This is a movie play.

## The true self and the free ego

The phrase “true self” can point in two different directions.

Theoretically, it can mean the one.

Not a person.
Not a personality.
Not an improved identity.
Not something inside time.

It is the thing that does not exist as an object, will not exist as an object, did not happen, and will not happen.

It is not a future state that the ego can reach.
It is not an event in the movie.
It is closer to the condition in which all events appear.

But practically, “true self” means something much more ordinary:

you.

Even when the one is recognized, the ego is still happening.
The body is still there.
The nervous system is still there.
The personal history is still there.
Habits, preferences, humor, fear, taste, love, fatigue, and responsibility are still there.

There is still a live person.

The realization does not erase the character from the movie.
It only changes the belief that the character is the final owner of the movie.

When the ego truly sees the one, it can become free in a practical sense.

Not free as in perfect.
Not free as in detached from life.
Not free as in permanently calm.

Free as in less covered.

The useless layers can start to fall away:

borrowed knowledge,
cultural background mistaken for identity,
old constraints,
defensive postures,
images created for survival,
and the pressure to become someone spiritually impressive.

What remains is not the absolute self.
It is still a pattern in the dream.

But it may be a more functional self.
A more honest self.
A self that can choose what to do, and also choose what constraints it actually needs.

This free ego could behave in many ways.
It depends on the constitution of that ego.
One person may become quiet.
Another may become playful.
Another may build things.
Another may raise a child, write code, make music, wash dishes, or do nothing special.

There is no single enlightened personality.
There is only this ego, with its real shape, no longer pretending so much.

In software terms, the system still runs its local process.
The process still has configuration, memory, limits, permissions, and runtime conditions.

Seeing the whole system does not delete the process.
It only prevents the process from believing it is the whole machine.

So the practical true self is not the one.
It is the ego after unnecessary abstraction is removed.

But this should not be misunderstood.

Even the free self is still a dream.
Even the honest personality is still part of the movie.
Even the ego that has seen through ego is still an appearance.

Freedom does not make the character ultimately real.
It only makes the character lighter.

## Reality addiction

A game addict treats things inside a game as if they are as precious as things in ordinary life.

A rank, an item, a virtual house, a score, a character skin, a win streak — all of these can become emotionally loaded.

From inside the game, they feel serious.
They feel worth protecting.
They feel worth fighting for.
They feel like they can define who the player is.

But from outside the game, the structure is easier to see.

It is still only a game.

Ordinary reality may work in a similar way.

This does not mean life is fake in a careless sense.
Pain still hurts.
People still matter.
Responsibilities still exist.
The body still needs food, sleep, warmth, and care.

But the mind can become addicted to reality in the same way it becomes addicted to a game.

It treats every gain and loss as ultimate.
It treats status as self.
It treats possessions as identity.
It treats praise, shame, success, failure, money, reputation, and even spiritual understanding as precious loot inside the game.

Then the character becomes too serious.

The player forgets that the character is happening in the game.
The ego forgets that the ego is happening in reality.
The process forgets that it is only a local process in a much larger system.

In that sense, ordinary attachment is a kind of reality addiction.

Not because reality should be hated.
Not because nothing matters.
Not because the body and the world should be neglected.

But because the extra seriousness is optional.

A game can be played sincerely without being worshiped.
A story can be lived fully without being mistaken for the final truth.
A character can be cared for without being treated as the absolute owner of the whole screen.

Maybe this is the lighter way:

Play the game.
Take care of the character.
Do the necessary quests.
Help other characters when possible.
Enjoy beauty when it appears.
Respond to pain when it appears.

But do not be too into it.

It is not worth giving the whole heart to the inventory of the game.
It is not worth turning every temporary scene into an ultimate identity.
It is not worth forgetting the screen because the movie became intense.

Reality continues.
The character continues.
The quests continue.

But the grip can loosen.

The world can be taken seriously enough to function,
and lightly enough not to be trapped by it.
